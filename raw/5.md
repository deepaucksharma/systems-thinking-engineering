## Section 1: Architecture

### 1.1 The Two-Level Design

**Narrative level** (for humans, executives, coaching):
```
OUTCOME = Strategic × Human × System × Learning × Debt/Risk
```

**Diagnostic level** (for modeling, measurement, root cause):
```
OUTCOME = V(t) · P(t) · E(t) · A(t) · L(t) · e^(-λ·Debt) · g(Var)
```

The mapping between levels:

| Narrative Layer | Diagnostic Blocks |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Strategic | V + strategic pieces of A |
| Human | P |
| System | E + structural pieces of A |
| Learning | L (multiplier on all three) |

**Why two levels?**
The narrative is what you communicate. The diagnostic blocks are what you actually use to find problems. One without the other is either too vague or too complex.

### 1.2 The Full Block Map

```
V(t) ─── Strategic Fit (SF)
      ├── Market/Problem Fit (CV)
      ├── Resource Alignment (RA)
      └── Strategic Decision Rights (A_strat)

P(t) ─── Psychological Safety (PS)
      ├── Motivation & Autonomy (M)
      ├── Fairness & Trust (FT)
      ├── Sustainability (Su)
      └── Lived Culture/Norms (Cu)

E(t) ─── Clarity (C)          ← OM1
      ├── Focus (F)            ← OM2
      ├── Skill (S)            ← OM3
      ├── Coordination (Co)    ← OM4
      ├── Quality (Q)          ← OM5
      └── Flow Variability     ← penalty term

A(t) ─── Incentive Alignment (IA)
      ├── Decision Rights & Power (DR)
      ├── Team Composition & Roles (TC)
      └── Cross-Team Architecture (XT)

L(t) ─── Feedback Speed (FS)  ← OM6 (relocated here)

```

**Note on OM6 (Feedback Speed):** In V1, Feedback Speed was a multiplier on the Execution equation. In V2 it becomes L's primary sub-variable. The mathematical effect is the same — it multiplies the whole — but its conceptual home is Learning, which is more honest about what it does.

## Section 2: The Master Equation

### 2.1 Full Form

$$\text{Output}(t) = \theta \cdot V(t) \cdot P(t) \cdot E(t) \cdot A(t) \cdot L(t) \cdot e^{-\lambda \cdot \text{Debt}(t)} \cdot g_{\text{var}}(\text{Var}(t))$$

### 2.2 Block Equations

Each block is internally multiplicative with softening weights so no sub-variable alone fully collapses the block (except for designated hard zeros):

$$V(t) = \prod_{i \in \{SF, CV, RA\}} \left(w_i \cdot v_i(t) + (1-w_i)\right) \cdot A_{\text{strat}}(t)$$

$$P(t) = Su(t) \cdot \prod_{j \in \{PS, M, FT, Cu\}} \left(w_j \cdot p_j(t) + (1-w_j)\right)$$

$$E(t) = \prod_{k \in \{C, F, S, Co, Q\}} \left(w_k \cdot e_k(t) + (1-w_k)\right) \cdot g_{\text{var}}(\text{Var}(t))$$

$$A(t) = \prod_{l \in \{IA, DR, TC, XT\}} \left(w_l \cdot a_l(t) + (1-w_l)\right)$$

$$L(t) = FS(t) \cdot \prod_{m \in \{LP, KR, CA\}} \left(w_m \cdot l_m(t) + (1-w_m)\right)$$

**Note on Sustainability (Su):** Su sits outside the P product term as a direct multiplier because when Su < 1.0, ALL conditions decay over time — not just People conditions. Su is the system's clock-speed modifier.

### 2.3 Debt and Variability Terms

**Debt drag:**
$$e^{-\lambda \cdot \text{Debt}(t)}$$

Where Debt(t) bundles three types:
- **Tech Debt:** Code quality, architecture, infrastructure
- **Process Debt:** Workarounds, broken rituals, exception-laden workflows
- **Org Debt:** Misaligned teams, unowned systems, governance gaps, title inflation

Each accumulates silently and compounds. Org Debt is the most dangerous because it is least visible.

**Variability penalty:**
$$g_{\text{var}}(\text{Var}(t)) = 1 - \left(\frac{\sigma}{\mu}\right)^2$$

When coefficient of variation > 1.0, variability effects dominate mean effects. Two teams with identical average throughput but different variance are fundamentally different systems. The one with high variance is experiencing a different — and worse — reality than its averages suggest.

### 2.4 The Gating Interactions

These are the most important nonlinearities. They cannot be read from block scores alone:

**Gate 1: Learning is gated by Safety**
$$L_{\text{eff}}(t) = L(t) \cdot PS(t)$$

Fast feedback loops with low psychological safety don't accelerate learning — they accelerate fear-driven distortion. Retrospectives become theater. Postmortems become blame rituals. Every signal becomes politically filtered before it reaches the people who need it.

**Gate 2: Execution is gated by Org Alignment**
$$E_{\text{eff}}(t) = E(t) \cdot A(t)$$

A team that is penalized for saying no, rewarded for heroics over prevention, and blocked by cross-team dependencies cannot sustainably execute — even if their process is perfect. The org game overrides the team game.

**Gate 3: Strategic Value is gated by Learning**
$$V_{\text{eff}}(t) = V(t) \cdot L(t)$$

In high-uncertainty domains (most software product work), strategy without experimentation is mostly prior belief. The quality of strategic decisions decays rapidly without feedback from reality. A plan that cannot be tested cannot be improved.

**Gate 4: All decay rates are gated by Sustainability**

When Su < 1.0, every condition decays at an accelerated rate:
$$\frac{d}{dt}[\text{any condition}] \propto -(1 - Su(t))$$

A burned-out team doesn't just perform worse today. It loses ground on every dimension simultaneously — skill atrophies, coordination erodes, quality standards slip, learning stops. Sustainability is the system's immune system.

## Section 3: Block Definitions — Complete Reference

### Block V: Value Alignment

**Core question:** If we executed this work perfectly, would it matter?

| Sub-variable | Definition | Zero state | Observable signal |
|-------------|------------|------------|------------------|
| Strategic Fit (SF) | Work maps to coherent, non-contradictory strategic hypothesis | Conflicting mandates, "strategy du jour," no unifying logic | Priority reversals per quarter, strategy document age |
| Market/Problem Fit (CV) | Evidence that outputs solve real problems for real users | Building features nobody uses, internal metrics diverge from user behavior | Usage rates, user interview frequency, outcome vs output ratio |
| Resource Alignment (RA) | Investment actually matches stated strategy | Strategic work starved by urgent/reactive work, roadmap vs actual allocation diverge | % time on strategic vs reactive, roadmap adherence |
| Strategic Decision Rights (A_strat) | Leaders rewarded for sustainable value creation, not short-term optics | Promotions go to firefighters, prevention is invisible | Who got promoted for what in last 18 months |

**Critical distinction from Clarity (E1):**
- Clarity: "Does the team know what they're trying to do?"
- Strategic Fit: "Is what they're trying to do worth doing?"

You can have perfect Clarity around a bad objective. A team with SF = 0 is optimizing execution of waste.

**Time constant:** Quarters to years. Strategy changes are expensive and slow to propagate.

---

### Block P: People System

**Core question:** Can the humans in this system tell the truth, sustain performance, and actually want to do the work?

| Sub-variable | Definition | Zero state | Observable signal |
|-------------|------------|------------|------------------|
| Psychological Safety (PS) | Safety to surface bad news, uncertainty, disagreement, mistakes | Retaliation for honesty, "shooting the messenger," filtered upward reporting | Time between problem occurring and being raised; skip-level divergence from direct reports |
| Motivation & Autonomy (M) | Intrinsic drive; experience of agency, mastery, purpose | Learned helplessness, quiet quitting, compliance without engagement | Self-initiated improvements, discretionary effort, voluntary attrition of high performers |
| Fairness & Trust (FT) | Perceived justice in decisions, rewards, and information flow | Gaming, cynicism, coalition-building instead of problem-solving | Unwritten rules vs stated rules divergence; political behavior as primary career strategy |
| Sustainability (Su) | Recovery rate ≥ depletion rate; cognitive load manageable | Systemic burnout, sick leave spikes, error rate increase, emotional flatness | Energy surveys (week N vs N-4); sick leave trends; after-hours work patterns |
| Lived Culture/Norms (Cu) | Actual day-to-day norms align with stated values | "We value quality" but people are punished for slowing down; "we value learning" but postmortems blame individuals | Stated values vs observed behavior delta; what actually gets praised in team meetings |

**The Safety Substrate:**
When PS → 0, all other measurements become unreliable. Survey responses are filtered. Metrics are gamed. Retrospectives produce consensus lies. You are flying blind with instruments that report false readings.

**The Sustainability Clock:**
Su is the only sub-variable that acts as a system-wide multiplier on decay rates. When Su < 0.7, you are consuming the team's future capacity to produce today's output. The accounting will come due.

**Time constants:**
- Safety: Can collapse in days (single bad incident); rebuilds over months
- Burnout: Accumulates over months; recovery takes equal time or longer
- Culture: Years in both directions

### Block E: Execution System

**Core question:** Can work actually flow through this team from intention to delivered value?

This is the original V1 framework, preserved intact. It is now properly contextualized as the execution layer — necessary but not sufficient.

| Sub-variable | Definition | Zero state | Observable signal |
|-------------|------------|------------|------------------|
| Clarity (C) | Shared understanding of goals, priorities, and success criteria | Team members give different answers about what they're trying to achieve | Goal recall variance, priority churn rate |
| Focus (F) | Protected attention; work-in-progress within capacity | Everyone doing everything simultaneously, constant context switching | WIP/capacity ratio, unplanned work percentage |
| Skill (S) | Capability to solve the technical problems the team actually faces | Stuck on every problem; solutions require heroics every time | Inverse problem resolution time, complexity-adjusted delivery rate |
| Coordination (Co) | Work flows without collisions, duplications, or gaps | Constant rework from misalignment, handoff failures, ownership disputes | Blocker duration/total work, handoff failure rate |
| Quality (Q) | Output meets standards without rework | Defect avalanche, escaped bugs, perpetual firefighting | Escaped defect rate, rework percentage |

**Fix-order (within E, after higher blocks are checked):**
C → F → S/Co (check A first) → Q → FS

**The original theorems still hold inside E:**
- Losing Loop: Focus → 0 triggers the self-reinforcing degradation cycle
- Winning Loop: Focus > 0 with slack enables the self-reinforcing improvement cycle
- Zero-Override: A zero in any E condition is treated first regardless of fix-order
- Structure-vs-Development: When you can't distinguish Skill from Coordination failures, fix Coordination (Structure) first

**What changes from V1:** Before concluding an E problem is an E problem, verify that A is not suppressing E. Many persistent Clarity failures are actually strategic incoherence (V). Many persistent Focus failures are actually incentive failures (A). Many persistent Quality failures are actually sustainability failures (P).

**Time constant:** Sprints to months.

### Block A: Org Alignment & Structure

**Core question:** Does the organizational system permit effective work, or does it structurally prevent it?

| Sub-variable | Definition | Zero state | Observable signal |
|-------------|------------|------------|------------------|
| Incentive Alignment (IA) | Actual rewards match behaviors that produce sustainable value | Heroics are celebrated, prevention is invisible; short-term output rewarded over long-term quality | Who got promoted and for what; what gets praised in public forums |
| Decision Rights & Power (DR) | Decisions made at the right level, by people with context and accountability | Either everything escalates (no autonomy) or nothing escalates (no accountability); HiPPO culture | Decision latency; escalation rate; post-decision reversal rate |
| Team Composition & Roles (TC) | Seniority mix, role clarity, ownership boundaries, succession depth | Everyone asks the same two people everything; role collisions on every handoff; key-person risk | Bus factor, role ambiguity survey, time spent on unowned work |
| Cross-Team Architecture (XT) | Team boundaries match technical architecture; dependencies are managed | Team A blocked by Team B monthly; shared services are constant bottlenecks; Conway's Law violations | Cross-team blocker frequency, shared service queue depth, release coordination cost |

**Why A is often the real problem:**
Most "team performance" problems that resist team-level interventions are actually A problems. You cannot fix Focus by telling the team to focus if their manager's manager is rewarding responsiveness to every urgent request. You cannot fix Coordination by improving team processes if the architecture requires constant cross-team negotiation.

**Conway's Law as a diagnostic:**
When XT is low, look at the architecture. The org chart IS the architecture. You cannot impose technical architecture that contradicts organizational structure. The Inverse Conway Maneuver: if you want a different architecture, reorganize the teams first.

**Time constant:** Years. Reorgs and incentive changes are the slowest interventions in the system. This means A problems require the most lead time and the most executive commitment to fix.

### Block L: Learning & Adaptation

**Core question:** Does the system improve over time, or does it repeat the same patterns indefinitely?

| Sub-variable | Definition | Zero state | Observable signal |
|-------------|------------|------------|------------------|
| Feedback Speed (FS) | Time from action to consequential signal (technical and product) | Discovering bugs in production months after introduction; user behavior unknown until launch | Lead time to defect detection, time to user signal, CI/CD cycle time |
| Learning Practices (LP) | Retrospectives, postmortems, experimentation, knowledge sharing are effective | Retros produce no changes; postmortems blame individuals; no experimentation discipline | Action item completion rate, behavior changes from retros, experiment velocity |
| Knowledge Retention (KR) | Organizational memory survives personnel changes | Same mistakes repeated; every new person starts from zero; bus factor = 1 | Onboarding time trend, documentation coverage, repeat incident rate |
| Change Absorption (CA) | Rate of change in process/structure is within system's capacity to integrate | Process thrash; new initiatives before old ones land; teams don't know which model to follow | Number of simultaneous initiatives, process change frequency vs Nyquist constraint |

**The Nyquist Constraint applies to L:**
Change Absorption (CA) is low when the rate of imposed change exceeds the system's natural frequency. Specifically: do not intervene faster than feedback propagates. Wait 2 full cycles before judging any intervention. When managers change strategy faster than feedback propagates, the manager IS the instability source.

**The critical interaction:**
$$L_{\text{eff}} = L(t) \cdot PS(t)$$

Fast feedback loops with low psychological safety are dangerous. They produce:
- Retrospectives that identify symptoms but not causes (honesty is unsafe)
- Postmortems that become blame rituals (psychological safety = 0)
- Experiments that are never truly negative (admitting failure is career risk)
- Organizational memory that records the official story, not what actually happened

The learning system only works if the people system permits honesty.

**Time constant:** Medium — weeks to months if P and A are supportive; indefinitely slow if they are not.

## Section 4: Diagnosis Playbook

### 4.1 The Extended Zero-Override Set

**Rule:** If any of the following are near zero, treat it as an emergency override of the default diagnostic sequence. These are structural failures that make lower-level interventions futile or misleading.

| Hard Zero | Condition | Why It Overrides |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| SF ≈ 0 | No credible strategy or contradictory mandates | Execution optimization is waste production |
| PS ≈ 0 | Psychological safety collapse | All other measurements are unreliable |
| Su ≈ 0 | Systemic burnout | System is consuming future capacity; will collapse |
| IA ≈ 0 | Incentives reward harmful behavior | Every correct local intervention will be reversed by incentive pressure |
| XT ≈ 0 | Cross-team deadlock | Team-level fixes cannot overcome structural dependency failures |

**Protocol for hard zeros:**
1. Document it explicitly as an org-level constraint
2. Do not promise local interventions will produce large output gains
3. Escalate with a clear causal statement: "We are in a state where [X ≈ 0]; local optimization is bounded by [specific mechanism]"
4. Apply minimal local mitigations to prevent further degradation while escalation proceeds

### 4.2 The Default Diagnostic Sequence

When no hard zero dominates, proceed in layer order:

```
ENTRY
  ↓
STEP 0: Check for no-win conditions (V, PS, Su, IA, XT)
  → Any hard zero? → ESCALATE (protocol above)
  ↓
STEP 1: Verify People System (P) integrity
  → PS low? → Qualitative methods only; fix safety before metrics
  → Su low? → Reduce load immediately; system is eating itself
  ↓
STEP 2: Diagnose Execution (E) with A-awareness
  → For each E condition, check if A is suppressing it
  → Apply OM1→OM5 fix-order within E
  ↓
STEP 3: If E problems persist, inspect Org Alignment (A)
  → Check IA, DR, TC, XT
  → A problems require org-level interventions; document lever access
  ↓
STEP 4: Optimize Learning (L) only after S/P/E/A are minimally stable
  → Faster feedback into a broken system accelerates failure
  → Check CA (change absorption) before adding more process
  ↓
MEASURE: Wait appropriate time constant before re-diagnosing
```

### 4.3 Time Constants and the Nyquist Constraint

Different blocks operate at fundamentally different speeds. Violating these time constants — either by expecting faster results or by re-intervening before feedback propagates — is a primary source of manager-induced instability.

| Block | Sub-variable | Time to See Leading Signal | Time to See Lagging Signal | Notes |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|-------|
| V | Strategic Fit | 1-2 quarters | 2-4 quarters | Strategy changes are slow and expensive |
| V | Market Fit | 4-8 weeks | 1-2 quarters | Experiment data is faster than market data |
| P | Psychological Safety | 1-2 weeks (decline), 4-8 weeks (rebuild) | 2-3 months | Asymmetric: faster to destroy than build |
| P | Sustainability/Burnout | 2-4 weeks accumulation | 2-3 months consequence | Lagging because burnout hides until it breaks |
| P | Culture | Years | Years | Most interventions underestimate this |
| E | Clarity | 1-2 sprints | 1-2 months | Fast if goals are genuinely changed |
| E | Focus | 1-2 weeks | 1 month | WIP changes visible quickly |
| E | Skill | 4-8 weeks | 2-3 months | Development is inherently slow |
| E | Quality | 2-4 weeks | 1-2 months | Detection latency matters here |
| A | Incentive Alignment | 3-6 months | 1-2 years | Behavior change lags policy change |
| A | Org Structure | 3-6 months | 6-18 months | Reorgs have long integration tails |
| A | Team Composition | 4-8 weeks | 2-3 months | Role clarity faster than seniority mix |
| L | Feedback Speed | 1-2 sprints | 1-2 months | Technical changes are fastest |
| L | Learning Practices | 4-6 weeks | 2-3 months | Habit formation takes time |
| L | Knowledge Retention | Months | Quarters | Visible only when tested |

**Nyquist Constraint applied:**
- E interventions: Wait minimum 2-3 sprints
- P interventions: Wait 4-6 weeks for Safety signals; 2-3 months for Burnout
- A interventions: Wait 3-6 months minimum; do not re-intervene earlier
- V interventions: Wait 1-2 quarters; measure leading signals at 4-6 weeks
- L interventions: Wait 4-6 weeks

## Section 5: State Machine — V2

### 5.1 The Order Parameter

In V2, the order parameter Φ is multi-dimensional:

$$\Phi(t) = [\Phi_V, \Phi_P, \Phi_E, \Phi_A, \Phi_L]$$

Each sub-score is a normalized composite of its block's sub-variables.

The **overall system state** is determined by the minimum sub-score (because multiplication means the weakest block dominates), but the **pattern** of sub-scores determines which interventions will work.

### 5.2 Extended State Table

| State | Pattern | Characteristics | Primary Risk |
|-------|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| **P1 Crisis** | Any Φ < 0.3 | Losing loop active; fires everywhere; zero slack | Irreversible attrition and debt accumulation |
| **P2 Stabilizing** | Worst Φ: 0.3-0.5 | Exiting crisis; fragile; fires reducing | Regression if load increases |
| **P3 Functional** | All Φ: 0.5-0.7 | Delivery works; limited improvement momentum | Slow decay without active investment |
| **P4 Thriving** | All Φ > 0.7 | Winning loop engaged; calm; improving | Complacency; decay of invisible conditions |
| **P5 Scaling Crisis** | Φ_E high, Φ_A/Φ_P declining | Growth is breaking coordination and culture | A and P cannot keep pace with E demands |
| **P6 Mixed** | Φ scores split across ranges | Subsystems in different states | Wrong intervention for wrong subsystem |

**New named states (V2 additions):**

| State | Pattern | Characteristics |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Wrong Thing Fast** | Φ_V low, Φ_E high | Efficient waste production; will discover too late |
| **Sweatshop** | Φ_P low, Φ_E medium-high | Unsustainable performance; burning people for output |
| **Good Intentions** | Φ_V high, Φ_P high, Φ_E low | Great culture, bad execution; can't deliver on vision |
| **Cynical Machine** | Φ_A low, Φ_E medium | Working hard inside a broken incentive system; rational disengagement |
| **Locally Optimal** | Φ_E high, Φ_V/Φ_A medium | Team is great; org game is wrong; team will eventually stop trying |

### 5.3 P6 Rule (Mixed State) — Extended

**Original rule:** Use worst state for stabilization, best state for investment.

**V2 extension:** Identify which block is driving the worst score. The intervention must match the block.

- Worst score in P: stabilize with psychological safety and load reduction
- Worst score in V: stabilize with scope reduction and strategy clarification
- Worst score in A: stabilize by documenting constraints and escalating; do not promise team-level fixes

Never average sub-scores across blocks. A team with Φ_P = 0.2 and Φ_E = 0.9 is not a "0.55 team." It is a high-executing team about to collapse due to burnout and fear.

## Section 6: Updated Losing and Winning Loops

### 6.1 V1 Loops — Preserved

The original loops operate inside the Execution block and are unchanged:

**Losing Loop (R1):**
```
Workload → Saturation → Context Switching → Quality Drop
→ Defects → Tech Debt → Rework → More Workload
```

**Winning Loop (R2):**
```
Slack → Investment Time → Skill ↑ + Coordination ↑
→ Quality ↑ → Throughput ↑ → Workload ↓ → More Slack
```

### 6.2 V2 Loops — Cross-Layer

**Incentive Death Spiral (cross-layer: A → P → E):**
```
Heroics Rewarded → Prevention Invisible → People Optimize for Heroics
→ Problems Not Fixed → More Heroics Required → Burnout
→ Sustainability ↓ → Quality ↓ → More Fires → More Heroics
```
Breaking requires: changing what gets visibly rewarded. Cannot be fixed at the team level.

**Fear Amplification Loop (cross-layer: P → L → V):**
```
Fear of Bad News → Problems Hidden → Feedback Distorted
→ Strategy Based on False Signal → Wrong Bets Made
→ Outcomes Fail → Blame Intensifies → More Fear
```
Breaking requires: blameless culture + leader modeling of truth-telling. Cannot be fixed by faster feedback alone.

**Sustainability Drain Loop (cross-layer: P → E → A):**
```
Overload → Burnout Accumulates → Quality ↓ + Learning Stops
→ Debt Accumulates → More Firefighting Required → More Overload
```
Breaking requires: genuine load reduction plus slack protection. Cannot be fixed by efficiency improvements alone.

**Strategic Drift Loop (cross-layer: V → L → V):**
```
No Experimentation → Strategy Based on Assumptions
→ Wrong Features Built → No Signal → Assumptions Harden
→ Investment in Wrong Direction → Market Fit ↓
```
Breaking requires: explicit experimentation budget and outcome-based strategy. Cannot be fixed by execution improvement.

## The V2 Theorems (Complete Set)

1. **Multiplicative Identity:** Output requires all five blocks. A zero in any block collapses output regardless of strength elsewhere.

2. **Measurement Reliability Theorem:** When PS < 0.5, all quantitative measurements are suspect. Qualitative methods become primary.

3. **Sustainability Clock Theorem:** When Su < 1.0, all conditions decay at rate proportional to (1 - Su). Current performance is being borrowed from future capacity.

4. **Variability Dominance Theorem:** When CV(cycle time) > 1.0, variability effects dominate mean effects. Reducing variance is more valuable than improving the mean.

5. **Gating Theorems:**
   - Learning effectiveness is gated by Safety: L_eff = L · PS
   - Execution effectiveness is gated by Org Alignment: E_eff = E · A
   - Strategic value realization is gated by Learning: V_eff = V · L

6. **Time Constant Hierarchy:** A > V > P(culture) > L > E > P(safety/burnout). Faster interventions cannot substitute for slower structural changes. Expecting A-speed changes from E-speed interventions is a primary source of manager frustration.

7. **Escalation Theorem:** When the root cause lives in A or V, team-level interventions are bounded. The manager's job becomes documentation and escalation, not local optimization.

8. **Nyquist Generalization:** Every block has a minimum observation window. Diagnosing or re-intervening before that window closes makes the manager the source of instability.

9. **Condition Decay (Entropy Theorem):** Without active maintenance, all conditions decay. Stability is not the natural state. The winning loop is not self-sustaining.

10. **Strategic Override:** Perfect execution of the wrong strategy produces zero customer value. V = 0 makes E irrelevant.
