## 1. Top-Level Identity

We treat sustainable engineering performance as:

```text
OUTPUT(t) ≈ V(t) · P(t) · E(t) · A(t) · L(t)
           · e^{−λ·Debt(t)} · g_tail(Var(t))
```

Where:

- **V(t)** – Value Alignment (strategy, customer value, domain-appropriate approach)  
- **P(t)** – People System (safety, motivation, fairness, cognitive sustainability, culture)  
- **E(t)** – Execution Physics (clarity, focus, skill, coordination, quality, flow + batch/queue dynamics)  
- **A(t)** – Org Alignment & Structure (incentives, power, decision rights, team design, sociotechnical architecture)  
- **L(t)** – Learning & Adaptation (feedback speed, institutional learning, change absorption, experimentation)  
- **Debt(t)** – technical + process + organizational debt (drag term)  
- **Var(t)** – variability / tail behaviour of flow (not just σ, but P95, obesity index, etc.)  
- θ – scale/domain constant

The **three-layer story** for non-technical audiences:

```text
Outcome = Strategic × Human × System × Learning × (Debt / Risk adjustments)
```

with:
- Strategic ≈ V + strategic slice of A  
- Human ≈ P  
- System ≈ E + structural slice of A  
- Learning ≈ L

## 2. Blocks and Key Sub-Variables

### 2.1 V(t) – Value Alignment (Strategic Layer)

“Are we doing the right work in the right way for this domain?”

Core components:

- **SF – Strategic Fit / Purpose**  
  Coherent mission and strategy; non-contradictory goals.  

- **CV – Customer / Problem Fit**  
  External validation: usage, retention, outcome achievement, revenue, mission metrics.  

- **RA – Resource Alignment**  
  Actual investment matches strategy (vs. starving strategic bets for urgent noise).  

- **DA – Domain Assessment (Cynefin)**  
  Correctly identifying Clear / Complicated / Complex / Chaotic and choosing constraint types accordingly.  

- **A_strat – Strategic Incentives & Decision Rights**  
  Top-level leaders rewarded for sustainable value, not short-term optics.

Effective strategic value:

```text
V_eff(t) = V(t) · f_domain(DA, strategy-type)
```

where f_domain < 1 when we apply “best practice” / fixed constraints in a Complex domain, or experiment-only in a Clear domain.

---

### 2.2 P(t) – People System (Human Layer)

“Can humans tell the truth, stay motivated, and sustain performance?”

Core components:

- **PS – Psychological Safety**  
  Truth-telling capacity; freedom to surface bad news and dissent.

- **M – Motivation & Autonomy (SDT)**  
  Autonomy, competence, relatedness; intrinsic vs controlled motivation.

- **F_t – Fairness & Trust**  
  Perceived justice in decisions, rewards, promotions.

- **Su – Sustainability / Burnout**  
  Balance of demands vs resources; energy and cognitive reserves.

- **Cu – Lived Culture / Underlying Assumptions**  
  What actually gets punished/rewarded (Schein’s deepest layer).

- **EL – Extraneous Load**  
  Friction: interruptions, broken pipelines, noisy alerts, bureaucratic overhead.

- **GC – Germane Capacity**  
  Protected time/energy for learning, reflection, mastery.

We can think of:

```text
P(t) ≈ Su · PS · M · F_t · Cu · (1 − EL) · GC
```

Measurement frame: **SPACE**  
- S(atisfaction) → Su, M  
- P(erformance) → outcomes from E  
- A(ctivity) → flow metrics, but not over-weighted  
- C(ommunication) → PS, Cu, Coord  
- E(fficiency) → EL, Focus, deep-work hours

---

### 2.3 E(t) – Execution Physics (Team-Level System Layer)

“Can work flow through this team under real-world variability?”

Core components (original OM1–OM5 plus refinements):

- **C – Clarity**  
- **F – Focus / WIP**  
- **S – Skill**  
- **Co – Coordination (local)**  
- **Q – Quality**

Add explicit flow physics:

- **BS – Batch Size**  
  Size of work items relative to capacity (epics vs small slices).

- **Util – Utilization**  
  Actual load vs capacity; must stay well below “physics cliff”.

- **TR – Tail Risk**  
  P95/P99 cycle time, Obesity Index; shape of distribution, not just mean.

- **Var – Variability**  
  Arrival and service-time variability across the pipeline.

We can write:

```text
E(t) = C · F · S · Co · Q · g_batch(BS, Util) · g_tail(TR) 
```

Where:
- g_batch decreases sharply with large batch size and high utilization (M[X]/M/1/∞ logic).  
- g_tail captures reliability penalties from heavy tails (e.g., via P95/μ and Obesity Index).

---

### 2.4 A(t) – Org Alignment & Structure (Org-Level System Layer)

“Does the org’s structure, incentives, and architecture allow good execution to exist?”

Core components:

- **IA – Incentive Alignment**  
  What actually gets rewarded (heroics vs prevention, quality vs speed).

- **DR – Decision Rights & Power**  
  Who can say no, who decides what, with what frameworks (RACI, DACI, RAPID).

- **TC – Team Composition & Roles**  
  Seniority mix, T-shaped skills, bus factor, onboarding path (no fixed “30/40/30” law, but composition sanity).

- **XT – Cross-Team Architecture (Conway)**  
  Alignment between team structure and technical architecture.

- **TT – Team Topology Health**  
  Clear use of Stream-aligned, Enabling, Complicated Subsystem, Platform teams where appropriate.

- **IM – Interaction Modes**  
  Correct mix of Collaboration, X-as-a-Service, and Facilitation between teams.

- **PQ – Platform Quality**  
  Platform teams actually reducing extraneous load (EL) vs adding bureaucracy.

A(t) is the reason some execution problems cannot be solved locally.

---

### 2.5 L(t) – Learning & Adaptation

“Do we get better with time, or just keep repeating?”

Core components:

- **FS – Feedback Speed**  
  Time from action to signal (tech + product).

- **LP – Learning Practices**  
  Retros, postmortems, incident reviews, experimentation discipline.

- **KR – Knowledge Retention**  
  Documentation, onboarding speed, organizational memory.

- **CA – Change Absorption (J-curve aware)**  
  How much change we can absorb before thrash; tolerance for the transition dip.

- **ER – Experimentation Rate**  
  Especially in Complex domains: safe-to-fail experiments per unit time.

Crucial gating:

```text
L_eff(t) = L(t) · PS
```

When safety is low, feedback is noise or lies.

## 3. Gating Relationships and Time Constants

### 3.1 Gating (Nonlinearity)

Key interaction rules:

- **Learning gated by People**

  ```text
  L_eff ≈ L · PS  (low PS ⇒ effective learning ≈ 0)
  ```

- **Execution gated by Org Alignment**

  ```text
  E_eff ≈ E · A   (perverse incentives or bad topology cap E)
  ```

- **Strategy gated by Domain & Learning**

  ```text
  V_eff ≈ V · L · f_domain(DA)
  ```

- **Decay gated by Sustainability**

  ```text
  higher (1−Su) ⇒ faster decay of V, P, E, A, L over time
  ```

### 3.2 Time Constants / Nyquist by Layer

Approximate feedback horizons:

- **V (strategy, domain choice):** quarters–years  
- **A (org structure, incentives):** quarters–years  
- **E (team processes, WIP, batch size):** sprints–months  
- **P (human state):** can degrade in days; recovery in weeks–months  
- **L (learning practices):** weeks–months

**Rule:** don’t judge an intervention or re-intervene faster than 2× that layer’s feedback horizon, and explicitly expect a **J-curve dip** in E and P when making non-trivial A or V changes.

## 4. Diagnostic Rules (V2.1 Playbook)

Keep Zero-Override and Fix-Order, now with added checks.

### 4.1 Hard Zero-Override

Treat as “hard zeros” requiring escalation above the team:

- V ≈ 0 – No credible strategy or real problem to solve.  
- PS ≈ 0 – Psychological safety collapse.  
- Su ≈ 0 – Systemic burnout.  
- IA ≈ 0 – Incentives reward harmful behaviour.  
- XT/TT ≈ 0 – Critical cross-team deadlock or hopeless topology.  

Do **not** treat these as local execution problems.

### 4.2 Default Fix-Order (When No Hard Zero)

1. **Strategic / Value (V + A_strat)**  
   - SF, CV, RA, DA: Are we aimed at something real, in the right way for this domain?

2. **People / Human (P)**  
   - PS, Su first; then M, F_t, Cu, EL/GC: Is the human system sane and sustainable?

3. **System / Execution & Structure (E + structural A)**  
   - Apply OM1–OM5, but:
     - **Batch Size Check:** are “items” actually huge epics?  
     - **Tail Risk Check:** is P95/P99 terrible despite OK averages?  
     - Check IA, DR, TC, XT, TT, IM, PQ behind recurring E problems.

4. **Learning (L)**  
   - FS, LP, KR, CA, ER: Are we learning at the right rate for our domain and change load?

### 4.3 New Explicit Rules

- **Batch Size Rule:**  
  Don’t just cap WIP; also aggressively shrink batch size. WIP=3 with 6-month epics is worse than WIP=10 with 1-week stories.

- **Tail Risk Rule:**  
  If average metrics look fine but reliability is poor, inspect P95/P99 and tail heaviness (Obesity Index). Plan and commit using tail-aware metrics, not means.

- **Domain Calibration Rule:**  
  Diagnose Cynefin domain before picking intervention type. Don’t apply rigid best practices in Complex work; don’t treat Clear work as if it were research.

- **Cognitive Load Decomposition Rule:**  
  When “cognitive load is too high,” ask:
  - Intrinsic? → increase skill, change team composition.  
  - Extraneous (EL)? → automate, fix tooling, cut meetings.  
  - Germane (GC low)? → create slack for learning.

- **J-Curve Rule:**  
  For any major change, explicitly expect and communicate an initial performance dip; set observation windows accordingly and avoid thrashing the system mid-dip.

### `00-model-identity.md` — The One-Page Summary

# Engineering Organization Control Theory: Model Identity

## What This Is

A diagnostic and intervention framework for engineering organizations, built on the principle that **output is multiplicative across five conditions**. A zero in any condition collapses everything. This is arithmetic, not opinion.

## The Master Equation

```
Output(t) = θ · V(t) · P(t) · E(t) · A(t) · L(t) · e^(-λ·Debt) · g(Var)
```

| Block | Question | Zero State |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **V** Value Alignment | Are we building the right thing? | Perfect execution of wrong goal |
| **P** People System | Can humans tell truth and sustain? | Fear and burnout distort everything |
| **E** Execution System | Can work actually flow? | Team cannot deliver regardless of intent |
| **A** Org Alignment | Does the org permit this to work? | Local fixes are structurally futile |
| **L** Learning | Do we improve or just repeat? | Spinning without getting better |

## The Diagnostic Sequence

```
Step 0: Check for no-win conditions (V, PS, Su, IA, XT near zero)
Step 1: Verify People System integrity (Safety first; then Sustainability)
Step 2: Diagnose Execution (OM1→OM5) with A-awareness
Step 3: If E problems persist, inspect Org Alignment (A)
Step 4: Optimize Learning only after S/P/E/A are minimally stable
```

## The Three Rules That Override Everything

1. **Zero-Override:** A near-zero anywhere is fixed before anything else, regardless of fix-order.
2. **Gating:** Learning is gated by Safety. Execution is gated by Org Alignment. Strategy is gated by Learning.
3. **Nyquist:** Every block has a minimum observation window. Re-intervening before that window closes makes the manager the instability source.

## The Most Important Insight

**The measurement system is itself a variable.** When Psychological Safety (PS) is low, all metrics become unreliable. You are flying blind with instruments that report false readings. Fix the measurement system before trusting the measurements.

### `02-master-equation.md` — The Full Equation

# The Master Equation

## Full Form

$$\text{Output}(t) = \theta \cdot V(t) \cdot P(t) \cdot E(t) \cdot A(t) \cdot L(t) \cdot e^{-\lambda \cdot \text{Debt}(t)} \cdot g_{\text{tail}}(\text{Var}(t))$$

This is a **conceptual identity**, not a calculator. Its purpose is to make explicit:
- Which conditions matter
- That they multiply (not add)
- That a zero anywhere collapses the product
- That gating relationships exist between blocks

## Block Equations

### Value Alignment

$$V(t) = f(SF, CV, RA, DA) \cdot A_{\text{strat}}$$

| Variable | Name | Definition |
|----------|------|------------|
| SF | Strategic Fit | Coherent, non-contradictory strategy |
| CV | Customer/Problem Value | Evidence of real user need |
| RA | Resource Alignment | Investment matches stated strategy |
| DA | Domain Assessment | Correct Cynefin domain identification |
| A_strat | Strategic Decision Rights | Leaders rewarded for sustainable value |

### People System

$$P(t) = Su(t) \cdot PS \cdot M \cdot FT \cdot Cu \cdot (1 - EL)$$

| Variable | Name | Definition |
|----------|------|------------|
| Su | Sustainability | Recovery rate ≥ depletion rate |
| PS | Psychological Safety | Safe to surface reality without retribution |
| M | Motivation & Autonomy | Intrinsic drive; agency; mastery |
| FT | Fairness & Trust | Perceived justice in decisions and rewards |
| Cu | Lived Culture | Actual norms vs stated values |
| EL | Extraneous Load | Friction consuming cognitive budget |

**Note:** Su multiplies directly — not inside the product term — because when Su < 1.0, ALL conditions decay at rate (1 - Su) per period.

### Execution System

$$E(t) = C \cdot F \cdot S \cdot Co \cdot Q \cdot g_{\text{batch}}(BS)$$

| Variable | Name | V1 Name | Definition |
|----------|------|---------|------------|
| C | Clarity | OM1 | Shared understanding of goals |
| F | Focus | OM2 | Protected attention; WIP within capacity |
| S | Skill | OM3 | Capability to solve actual problems |
| Co | Coordination | OM4 | Work flows without collision |
| Q | Quality | OM5 | Output meets standards first time |
| BS | Batch Size | — | Work item size relative to flow capacity |

### Org Alignment & Structure

$$A(t) = IA \cdot DR \cdot TC \cdot XT \cdot TT \cdot IM$$

| Variable | Name | Definition |
|----------|------|------------|
| IA | Incentive Alignment | Actual rewards match sustainable value behaviors |
| DR | Decision Rights | Decisions at right level, right accountability |
| TC | Team Composition | Seniority mix, role clarity, bus factor |
| XT | Cross-Team Architecture | Conway alignment; dependency management |
| TT | Team Topology Health | Teams are right type for their function |
| IM | Interaction Mode | Teams use appropriate interaction modes |

### Learning & Adaptation

$$L(t) = FS \cdot LP \cdot KR \cdot CA \cdot ER$$

| Variable | Name | V1 Name | Definition |
|----------|------|---------|------------|
| FS | Feedback Speed | OM6 | Time from action to consequential signal |
| LP | Learning Practices | — | Retros, postmortems, experiments are effective |
| KR | Knowledge Retention | — | Organizational memory survives personnel changes |
| CA | Change Absorption | — | Rate of change within system's integration capacity |
| ER | Experimentation Rate | — | Safe-to-fail experiments per period |

### Debt and Variability

$$\text{Debt drag} = e^{-\lambda \cdot \text{Debt}(t)}$$

Debt bundles:
- **Technical Debt:** Code, architecture, infrastructure
- **Process Debt:** Workarounds, exception-laden workflows
- **Org Debt:** Misaligned teams, unowned systems, governance gaps

$$\text{Variability penalty} = g_{\text{tail}}(\text{Var}) = f\left(\frac{P95}{\mu}, \text{Ob}(X)\right)$$

Where Ob(X) is the Obesity Index measuring tail heaviness class. Standard deviation understates tail risk in heavy-tailed distributions; P95/P99 and tail class are more honest.

## The Gating Relationships

These are the most important nonlinearities. They cannot be read from individual block scores.

### Gate 1: Learning is gated by Safety

$$L_{\text{eff}}(t) = L(t) \cdot PS(t)$$

**Meaning:** Fast feedback loops with low psychological safety don't accelerate learning — they accelerate fear-driven distortion. Every unit of L is multiplied by PS before it produces value.

**Diagnostic implication:** When L appears low, check PS first. Often the signal is there; the system is suppressing it.

### Gate 2: Execution is gated by Org Alignment

$$E_{\text{eff}}(t) = E(t) \cdot A(t)$$

**Meaning:** A team with correct process cannot sustainably execute if incentives reward the wrong behaviors or architecture requires constant cross-team negotiation. The org game overrides the team game.

**Diagnostic implication:** Persistent E failures that resist team-level intervention are usually A failures.

### Gate 3: Strategic Value is gated by Learning

$$V_{\text{eff}}(t) = V(t) \cdot L(t) \cdot f(DA)$$

**Meaning:** In Complex domains (most product work), strategy without experimentation is prior belief. The quality of strategic decisions degays rapidly without feedback from reality. Domain mismatch (applying best practices in Complex domains) multiplies the damage.

**Diagnostic implication:** When strategy seems right but outcomes are wrong, check L (learning from reality) and DA (domain-strategy alignment).

### Gate 4: Sustainability drives all decay rates

$$\frac{d}{dt}[\text{any condition}] \propto -(1 - Su(t))$$

**Meaning:** When Su < 1.0, every condition deteriorates. The system is consuming future capacity to produce present output. This accounting comes due.

**Diagnostic implication:** Apparent stability during high load periods is borrowed from the future. Leading indicators of Su degradation must be monitored even when E metrics look strong.

### `12-block-E.md` — Execution System (The Preserved Core)

# Block E: Execution System

## Core Question

*Can work actually flow from intention to delivered value through this team?*

## What This Block Is

Block E is the preserved core of the V1 model — the original five conditions (OM1-OM5) plus two additions from queuing physics: batch size and tail risk. It answers the purely mechanical question: given that strategy is valid, humans are healthy, and the org permits it — can this team actually execute?

**Block E is necessary but not sufficient.** All prior blocks must be checked before concluding a problem is an E problem.

## The Block Equation

$$E(t) = C \cdot F \cdot S \cdot Co \cdot Q \cdot g_{\text{batch}}(BS)$$

## The Five Conditions (OM1-OM5)

### Clarity (C) — OM1

**Definition:** Shared understanding of goals, priorities, and success criteria across the team.

**Zero state:** Team members give different answers about what they are trying to achieve. Work begins without a shared definition of done.

**Distinction from Strategic Fit (V block):** Clarity is "does the team know what they're trying to do?" Strategic Fit is "is what they're trying to do worth doing?" You can have perfect Clarity around a bad objective.

| Signal | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| Goal recall consistency | > 80% consistent | 60-80% | < 60% |
| Priority reversals per sprint | 0-1 | 2-3 | > 3 |
| "Done" defined before start | Always | Usually | Rarely |

**Before diagnosing Clarity failure, verify:**
- Is this actually a Strategic Fit (SF) problem? Conflicting mandates at the V level produce Clarity failures at the E level.
- Is this a Safety (PS) problem? When PS is low, teams know the real priority but won't say it.

**Intervention:** Define one goal per sprint in ≤ 25 words. Make priority explicit and forced-ranked. Kill ambiguous work before it starts.

---

### Focus (F) — OM2

**Definition:** Protected attention; work-in-progress within capacity; defense against fragmentation.

**Zero state:** Everyone is doing everything simultaneously. Context switching consumes all productive capacity. No slack exists to absorb variation.

**The Queuing Physics:** Focus is not just WIP count — it is the interaction of WIP count, batch size, and arrival variance. A team with WIP = 3 but each item is a 6-month epic is in a worse queuing state than a team with WIP = 10 with 3-day stories.

**The utilization curve:** At 85%+ utilization, cycle time goes nonlinear. At 95%+ utilization, cycle time becomes mathematically unbounded. The physics make 100% utilization a guarantee of infinite queue growth, not maximum productivity.

| Signal | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| WIP / capacity ratio | < 0.8 | 0.8-1.0 | > 1.0 |
| Unplanned work % | < 20% | 20-35% | > 35% |
| Context switches per engineer per day | < 2 | 2-4 | > 4 |
| Mean batch size vs team capacity | < 1 week | 1-4 weeks | > 4 weeks |

**Before diagnosing Focus failure, verify:**
- Is this an Incentive Alignment (IA) problem? If responsiveness is rewarded and WIP caps are punished, Focus cannot be fixed at the team level.
- Is this a Decision Rights (DR) problem? If the team cannot say no to incoming requests, WIP caps are theatrical.

**Intervention:** Set explicit WIP cap. Make unplanned work visible. Reduce batch size before reducing WIP count. Get DR clarity on who can authorize new work.

---

### Skill (S) — OM3

**Definition:** Capability to solve the technical problems the team actually faces — not generic skill, but specific problem-solving capacity for current challenges.

**Zero state:** Every problem requires heroics. The team is stuck for days on challenges that should take hours. External help is needed for routine work.

**The Composition Interaction:** Skill as an E-block variable measures the aggregate capability available. But the distribution of skill matters as much as the level. A team where all skill is concentrated in one person has low effective Skill despite high individual talent — bus factor = 1 means Skill can go to zero when that person is unavailable.

**Cognitive Load Interaction:** High extraneous cognitive load suppresses effective Skill. Engineers who spend 40% of their day on broken pipelines, noisy alerts, and bureaucratic friction have less cognitive capacity for actual technical problems.

| Signal | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| Inverse problem resolution time | Improving | Stable | Degrading |
| Problems requiring external help | < 10% | 10-25% | > 25% |
| Bus factor | > 3 | 2-3 | 1 |
| Onboarding time to productivity | Stable/declining | Slowly increasing | Rapidly increasing |

**Before diagnosing Skill failure, verify:**
- Is this a Team Composition (TC) problem? Wrong seniority mix is an A-block problem, not an E-block one.
- Is this a Coordination (Co) problem? Structure-vs-Development Rule: fix coordination before concluding you have a skill gap.

**Intervention:** Identify specific skill gaps, not generic "we need better engineers." Pair seniors with juniors on the actual problem domain. Reduce extraneous load to free cognitive capacity. Check TC before hiring.

---

### Coordination (Co) — OM4

**Definition:** Work flows without collisions, duplications, or gaps. Handoffs complete. Ownership is clear.

**Zero state:** Constant rework from misalignment. "I thought you owned that." Work falls through gaps between people. Handoff failures require weeks of recovery.

**The Architecture Interaction:** Many Coordination failures are not team process failures — they are architectural dependency failures. When team boundaries don't match technical architecture (Conway's Law violation), coordination overhead is structurally unavoidable. No amount of team process improvement can fix a Cross-Team Architecture (XT) problem.

**Structure-vs-Development Rule:** When you cannot distinguish Coordination failures from Skill failures, fix Coordination first. Coordination problems mask skill levels — a skilled team in a chaotic coordination system will appear less capable than it is.

| Signal | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| Blocker duration / total work | < 10% | 10-20% | > 20% |
| Handoff failure rate | < 5% | 5-15% | > 15% |
| "Who owns X?" resolution time | < 1 day | 1-3 days | > 3 days |
| Cross-team blocker frequency | < 1/sprint | 1-3/sprint | > 3/sprint |

**Before diagnosing Coordination failure, verify:**
- Is this a Cross-Team Architecture (XT) problem? If so, it's an A-block problem requiring org-level intervention.
- Is this a Decision Rights (DR) problem? Unclear ownership is often unclear authority, not unclear process.

**Intervention:** Map ownership before fixing process. If the same coordination failure keeps recurring, suspect XT. Apply Team Topologies (correct team type + interaction mode) before adding process.

### Quality (Q) — OM5

**Definition:** Output meets standards without rework. Defects are caught early. Technical debt is not silently accumulating.

**Zero state:** Defect avalanche. Perpetual firefighting. Every release requires emergency patches. The team spends more time fixing than building.

**The Sustainability Interaction:** Quality standards that cannot be met sustainably produce a specific failure mode: the team games quality metrics. When engineers are rewarded for speed and implicitly punished for slowing down, quality doesn't disappear — it goes underground. Bugs are shipped knowingly, tech debt accumulates invisibly, and the Quality metric reports green while the system quietly deteriorates toward avalanche.

**Self-Organized Criticality:** Technical debt accumulates invisibly until a critical threshold triggers cascading failure. There are no lagging indicators that predict this — the system looks stable until it doesn't. Only leading indicators (debt accumulation rate, escape rate trend, test coverage trend) provide warning.

| Signal | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| Escaped defect rate | < 1% | 1-3% | > 3% |
| Rework percentage | < 10% | 10-20% | > 20% |
| Technical debt accumulation rate | Declining | Stable | Growing |
| Time to detect defect after introduction | Hours | Days | Weeks |

**Before diagnosing Quality failure, verify:**
- Is this a Sustainability (Su) problem? Burned-out teams cannot maintain quality regardless of standards.
- Is this an Incentive Alignment (IA) problem? If speed is rewarded and quality investment is invisible, Quality cannot be fixed at the team level.

**Intervention:** Make quality investment visible and credited. Automate detection to reduce the cost of maintaining standards. Never grant quality exceptions — each exception establishes a new lower floor.

## The Batch Size Extension

**Why batch size is a separate variable from WIP:**

WIP count and batch size interact multiplicatively on cycle time. Reducing WIP from 10 to 5 while keeping batch size at 3 months improves cycle time less than reducing batch size from 3 months to 2 weeks while keeping WIP at 10.

The queuing model for software work is M[X]/M/1/∞ — batch-arrival, not single-arrival. This means:

$$\text{Cycle Time} \approx \frac{\text{WIP} \cdot \overline{\text{Batch Size}}}{\text{Throughput}} \cdot f(\text{Utilization}, \text{Arrival Variance})$$

**Batch size reduction is often the faster fix than WIP reduction** because:
- Smaller stories are easier to estimate (less variance)
- Smaller PRs are faster to review (reduces Co blocker time)
- Smaller releases fail more safely (reduces Q recovery cost)
- The non-linearity penalty drops faster with batch size reduction than WIP reduction at high utilization

**Practical heuristic:** If cycle time is high but WIP looks acceptable, check story size distribution. If P90 story size > 5 days, batch size is likely the primary driver.

---

## The Tail Risk Extension

**Why mean cycle time is insufficient:**

Software delivery times follow heavy-tailed distributions. The Obesity Index:

$$Ob(X) = P(X_1 + X_4 > X_2 + X_3 \mid X_1 \le X_2 \le X_3 \le X_4)$$

measures whether the tail of the distribution dominates the behavior of the sum.

**In heavy-tailed systems:**
- The mean is often mathematically meaningless (undefined or dominated by rare events)
- Standard deviation understates tail risk
- Two teams with identical mean and standard deviation can have radically different tail behavior
- Planning based on means produces systematic underestimation of catastrophic delays

**The diagnostic shift:**

| Planning Context | Use This Metric |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Daily team management | Mean cycle time |
| Sprint planning | P75 cycle time |
| Quarterly commitments | P90 cycle time |
| Stakeholder promises | P95 cycle time |
| Contractual commitments | P99 + explicit uncertainty |

**The Φ_E score must include both dimensions:**
- Mean performance component (how fast is typical delivery?)
- Tail risk component (how catastrophic are the worst cases?)

A team with low mean but fat tail is less reliable than their average suggests. This is the team that misses every quarterly commitment despite "looking fine" in weekly metrics.

# The Diagnostic Sequence

## Philosophy

Diagnosis proceeds from outermost constraints to innermost. Fixing the innermost problem while an outer constraint is violated produces local improvement that is immediately reversed by the outer constraint.

The sequence is not arbitrary — it reflects causal hierarchy:
- V failures make E success irrelevant (right execution, wrong goal)
- P failures make measurement unreliable (you cannot diagnose what you cannot measure honestly)
- A failures make E fixes futile (team process cannot override org incentives)
- L failures make everything permanent (you keep repeating the same mistakes)

## The Complete Decision Tree

```
ENTRY: Something is wrong, or you want to prevent something going wrong.

════════════════════════════════════════
STEP 0: CHECK FOR NO-WIN CONDITIONS
════════════════════════════════════════

Ask these five questions:

1. Is there a coherent, non-contradictory strategy?
   → If NO: SF (Strategic Fit) near zero. [GO TO: Zero-Override Protocol]

2. Are we building something plausibly valuable to real users?
   → If NO: CV (Customer Value) near zero. [GO TO: Zero-Override Protocol]

3. Can people surface bad news without retribution?
   → If NO: PS (Psychological Safety) near zero. [GO TO: Zero-Override Protocol]
   → CRITICAL: If NO, all subsequent measurements are unreliable.
              Switch to qualitative methods only.

4. Is there systemic burnout, sick-leave spike, or visible breaking?
   → If YES: Su (Sustainability) near zero. [GO TO: Zero-Override Protocol]

5. Are incentives actively rewarding harmful behavior?
   → If YES: IA (Incentive Alignment) near zero. [GO TO: Zero-Override Protocol]

6. Is there cross-team deadlock with no feasible path?
   → If YES: XT (Cross-Team Architecture) near zero. [GO TO: Zero-Override Protocol]

If any Step 0 condition fires:
→ Document as org-level constraint
→ Do NOT promise local interventions will produce large gains

→ Skip-level perception matches direct report perception?
→ Problems surface in days, weeks, or months?

Check 1b: Sustainability (Su)

→ Is there visible cynicism about decisions and rewards?

→ These require L-block improvements to address (retrospectives, visible credit)

════════════════════════════════════════

  ├── Symptom: WIP exploding, constant context switching, no slack
  ├── Check batch size first: Is WIP low but items are 3-month epics?
  ├── Check A first: Is IA rewarding responsiveness? Can DR say no?

  ├── Symptom: Work gets stuck, quality is inconsistent
  ├── Check XT first: Is this a cross-team architecture problem?
  ├── Check TC first: Is this a team composition (seniority mix) problem?
  ├── If Skill and Coordination both suspected: Fix Coordination first
  └── Only diagnose Skill gap after ruling out structural causes

  QUALITY (Q)
  ├── Symptom: Defect avalanche, perpetual firefighting, invisible debt
  ├── Check Su first: Burned-out teams cannot maintain standards
  ├── Check IA first: Are engineers penalized for slowing down for quality?
  ├── Check for silent gaming: Metrics green but system deteriorating?
  └── If neither: Automate detection, make debt visible, never grant exceptions

→ Who got promoted in the last 18 months, and for what?
→ What gets praised in all-hands and public forums?
→ Are heroics more visible than prevention?

If IA is the root cause:
→ Team-level fixes are bounded
→ Intervention requires: executive mandate, incentive redesign, visible crediting of prevention
→ Time constant: 6-18 months for behavior change

Check 3b: Decision Rights (DR)
→ Who can say no to new work entering the team?
→ Who can change priorities mid-sprint?
→ Are decisions made at the right level?

→ Clarify authority before fixing process
→ Apply DACI or RAPID to ambiguous decisions
→ Time constant: 3-6 months to establish new patterns

→ Is the seniority mix appropriate for current complexity?
→ Are teams the right type (stream-aligned, enabling, platform, subsystem)?
→ Are teams using the right interaction mode (collaboration, X-as-a-Service, facilitation)?

→ Treat as Inverse Conway Maneuver opportunity
→ Reorganize teams to match desired architecture, not vice versa

→ Recurring blocks from the same team?
→ Shared services that are permanent bottlenecks?
→ Release coordination consuming > 20% of sprint capacity?

→ Document and escalate as architectural debt
→ Apply Team Topologies framework
→ Time constant: 6-18 months for architectural restructuring

- Faster feedback into a broken execution system accelerates failure
- Heavy experimentation with low Su burns people
- New processes with low CA create thrash

Check 4a: Feedback Speed (FS)
→ Lead time from action to signal (technical and product)
→ Are defects detected in hours or weeks?
→ Is user behavior known in days or quarters?

Check 4b: Learning Practices (LP)
→ Do retros produce actual behavior changes?
→ Are postmortems blameless and systemic?
→ Is there structured experimentation with explicit hypotheses?

Check 4c: Knowledge Retention (KR)
→ Are the same mistakes being made repeatedly?
→ Does onboarding time trend up with team growth?
→ Is documentation current and findable?

Check 4d: Change Absorption (CA) — Apply Nyquist Constraint
→ How many simultaneous initiatives are active?
→ Was the last intervention given its full time constant before judgment?
→ If CA is low: stop adding new interventions; let existing ones land

MEASUREMENT WINDOW

2. What intervention is being applied?

5. What is the trigger for re-diagnosis?
```

### `06-time-constants.md` — Layer-Specific Nyquist

# Time Constants and the Nyquist Constraint

## The Core Principle

Every block operates at a different natural frequency. Intervening faster than a block's natural frequency — or measuring results before the feedback has propagated — makes the manager the source of instability.

**Nyquist Constraint:** Do not re-intervene until you have observed at least 2 full feedback cycles for that block.

## Time Constants by Block and Sub-Variable

| Block | Variable | Degradation Rate | Recovery Rate | Minimum Observation Window |
|-------|----------|-----------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| **V** | Strategic Fit (SF) | Months-quarters | Quarters-years | 1-2 quarters before judging |
| **V** | Customer Value (CV) | Weeks-months | Weeks-months (via experiments) | 4-8 weeks per experiment cycle |
| **V** | Domain Assessment (DA) | Fast (new info) | Fast (new info) | 2-4 weeks per learning cycle |
| **P** | Psychological Safety (PS) | **Days-weeks** | **Months** | 4-8 weeks to see early movement |
| **P** | Motivation (M) | Weeks-months | Weeks-months | 6-12 weeks |
| **P** | Sustainability/Burnout (Su) | Weeks-months | Equal to accumulation period | 4-8 weeks; recovery ≥ depletion time |
| **P** | Culture/Norms (Cu) | Years | **Years** | 12+ months; don't expect linear progress |
| **E** | Clarity (C) | Sprints | Sprints | 2-3 sprints |
| **E** | Focus (F) | Days-weeks | Weeks | 2-4 weeks |
| **E** | Skill (S) | Months | Months-quarters | 6-12 weeks |
| **E** | Quality (Q) | Weeks-months | Weeks-months | 4-8 weeks |
| **E** | Batch Size (BS) | Days-weeks | Days-weeks | 2-3 sprint cycles |
| **A** | Incentive Alignment (IA) | Months-years | **Months-years** | 6-18 months |
| **A** | Decision Rights (DR) | Months | Months-quarters | 3-6 months |
| **A** | Team Composition (TC) | Months | Quarters | 2-3 months per hire cycle |
| **A** | Cross-Team Architecture (XT) | Months-years | **Years** | 6-18 months |
| **L** | Feedback Speed (FS) | Weeks | Weeks | 2-4 weeks |
| **L** | Learning Practices (LP) | Weeks-months | Months | 6-10 weeks |
| **L** | Change Absorption (CA) | Days-weeks | Equal to overload period | Don't add new change during recovery |

## The Critical Asymmetry

**Safety (PS) has the most dangerous asymmetry:**
- Can be destroyed in days by a single high-visibility retaliatory event
- Requires months to rebuild even with consistent positive reinforcement
- This is why PS is treated as a hard zero requiring emergency response

**Culture (Cu) is the slowest:**
- Changes at the Schein "underlying assumptions" level take years
- Changes at the "artifacts" level take weeks and are often illusory
- Leaders systematically overestimate culture change speed

**Incentives (IA) lag behavior change:**
- Incentive policy can change overnight
- Behavior change follows at 3-6 months
- Culture internalization follows at 12-24 months
- Planning timelines must reflect this cascade

## The J-Curve: Transition Costs

Every significant intervention produces a temporary performance decrease before the new baseline is achieved.

```
Performance
    │
    │▓▓▓▓▓ Pre-intervention baseline
    │     ▓▓▓
    │        ▓ ← Transition dip (do not judge here)
    │         ▓▓
    │           ▓▓▓▓▓▓▓▓ Post-intervention baseline
    │
    └─────────────────────────────── Time
              ↑              ↑
         Intervention   Nyquist window
```

**Drivers of dip depth and duration:**

| Factor | Effect on Dip |
|--------|--------------|
| Change magnitude | Larger change = deeper dip |
| Extraneous cognitive load of new system | Higher friction = longer dip |
| Psychological Safety | Low PS = hidden confusion = extended dip |
| Change Absorption (CA) | Low CA = system overloaded = extended dip |

**J-Curve Protocol:**

Before implementing any significant intervention:
1. Predict the dip: estimate timing and depth based on change magnitude and CA
2. Communicate it: tell stakeholders explicitly "performance will temporarily decrease"
3. Set the Nyquist window: minimum observation period = J-curve duration + 1 feedback cycle
4. Define the abort signal: what pattern of leading indicators would indicate the dip is accelerating rather than recovering?

**The intervention failure pattern:**

Organizations measure during the dip, conclude the intervention failed, revert to baseline, and develop learned helplessness about change ("we tried that once"). This pattern is induced by:
- Not predicting the J-curve
- Not communicating it to stakeholders
- Measuring too early
- Not defining the abort signal in advance
